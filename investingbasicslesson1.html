What is Investing?

Investing is the act of putting your money into something—like stocks, bonds, or real estate—with the expectation of earning a return. Instead of letting your money sit in a savings account earning minimal interest, investing allows you to grow your wealth over time.

Examples:

  • Stocks: Buying shares of a company like Apple or Tesla.
  • Bonds: Lending money to a government or corporation in exchange for interest.
  • Real Estate: Owning property that can generate rental income or increase in value.

Why Should You Invest?

Saving money is great, but it’s not enough. Inflation—when the cost of goods and services rises—can erode the value of your money over time. Investing helps combat inflation and ensures your money grows faster than it loses value.

Real-Life Example:

Imagine you save $10,000 today. If inflation is 3% per year and your savings account earns 0.5% interest, your money will lose value. But if you invest it with an average annual return of 7%, your $10,000 could grow to $19,671 in 10 years!

Understanding Compound Interest

Compound interest is what makes investing so powerful. It's when your money earns interest, and then that interest earns interest too. The earlier you start, the more time your money has to grow.

Formula:

\( A = P(1 + r)^t \)
Where:
A: Future value
P: Initial investment
r: Annual interest rate
t: Time in years

Example:

If you invest $5,000 at 7% annual interest for 20 years, your money will grow to $19,348. Start 10 years later, and it only grows to $9,836.
